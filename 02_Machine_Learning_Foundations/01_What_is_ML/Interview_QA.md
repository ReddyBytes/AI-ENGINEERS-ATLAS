# What is ML — Interview Q&A

## Beginner Level

**Q1: What is Machine Learning in simple terms?**

<details>
<summary>💡 Show Answer</summary>

A: Machine Learning is a way of teaching computers by showing them examples instead of writing rules. The computer figures out the patterns itself.

</details>

<br>

**Q2: What's the difference between traditional programming and ML?**

<details>
<summary>💡 Show Answer</summary>

A: In traditional programming, you write the rules and the computer follows them. In ML, you give the computer data and the correct answers, and it learns the rules on its own.

</details>

<br>

**Q3: Give a real-world example of ML in use.**

<details>
<summary>💡 Show Answer</summary>

A: A spam filter. You don't manually write rules like "if the email contains 'win a prize' it's spam" — you show the model thousands of spam and non-spam emails and it learns to tell them apart.

</details>

<br>

**Q4: What are the 3 main types of Machine Learning?**

<details>
<summary>💡 Show Answer</summary>

A: Supervised learning (learns from labeled data), Unsupervised learning (finds patterns in unlabeled data), and Reinforcement learning (learns from rewards and penalties).

</details>

---

## Intermediate Level

**Q5: What is a "model" in ML?**

<details>
<summary>💡 Show Answer</summary>

A: A model is the output of the training process — it's the learned function that maps inputs to predictions. Think of it as the brain after studying.

</details>

<br>

**Q6: What's the difference between training and inference?**

<details>
<summary>💡 Show Answer</summary>

A: Training is when the model learns from data (adjusting its weights). Inference is when the trained model is used to make predictions on new, unseen data.

</details>

<br>

**Q7: What is a loss function?**

<details>
<summary>💡 Show Answer</summary>

A: A loss function measures how wrong the model's predictions are. During training, the goal is to minimize the loss — the lower the loss, the better the model.

</details>

<br>

**Q8: What is overfitting?**

<details>
<summary>💡 Show Answer</summary>

A: Overfitting is when a model memorizes the training data instead of learning general patterns. It performs great on training data but fails on new data — like a student who memorizes answers without understanding the concepts.

</details>

---

## Advanced Level

**Q9: How do you choose between ML and a rule-based system?**

<details>
<summary>💡 Show Answer</summary>

A: Use ML when: patterns are too complex to hand-code, you have large amounts of labeled data, or the patterns change over time. Use rule-based when: logic is simple and explainable, data is scarce, or you need 100% predictable behavior.

</details>

<br>

**Q10: Why does data quality matter more than data quantity?**

<details>
<summary>💡 Show Answer</summary>

A: A model learns patterns from its data. If the data is biased, mislabeled, or inconsistent, the model learns the wrong patterns — more of that bad data only amplifies the problem.

</details>
